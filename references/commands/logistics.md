## Registration & setup

Registration logistics are certification-specific and provider-specific: cost
varies by region, identity requirements differ, and test-center vs. online-
proctored setups have different prerequisites. This section gives you the
structured checklist and directs you to the canonical sources for your
specific exam.

### Before you register: prerequisites check

Confirm these facts from `exam-facts.md` (written by `discover`):

- **Exam code** — the provider's official identifier
- **Cost in your region** — `[OFFICIAL — <url> — retrieved YYYY-MM-DD]` from
  `exam-facts.md`, Cost & logistics section
- **Prerequisites** — any required certifications, hands-on experience, or

- **Delivery mode(s) available** — online proctored, test center, or both —

- **Your eligibility** — do you meet prerequisites, and is your region / role
  eligible? Ask the provider's official FAQ or eligibility checker before
  registering

If any of these fields are missing or marked "not verified" in `exam-facts.md`,
go back to `discover` and run it again before proceeding.

### Identity and document requirements

Every certification provider has specific ID requirements for registration and
exam day. These are **provider-specific facts, not generic**:

- Some require government-issued photo ID only (passport, driver's license)
- Others require government ID plus a secondary proof of residence
- Some certifications allow candidates to test in a native language other than
  English and require language-eligibility documentation
- Online-proctored exams often require stricter identity verification (recent
  selfie matching ID, in-person proctoring software checks)
- Test-center exams typically require photo ID on the day of the exam

**What to do:** Check your specific exam provider's official registration page
and exam-day checklist. These rules change by provider and region. Look for:

- "ID requirements" or "acceptable forms of identification" page
- "Exam day checklist" or "what to bring" section
- If test-center: the specific test center's rules (often stricter than the
  provider's minimum)
- If online-proctored: the proctoring software's system requirements and setup
  guide

Register with the exact name and date of birth matching your ID. Mismatches on
exam day can result in cancellation without refund.

### Online-proctored setup

If you've chosen online proctoring (or if your exam offers only that mode):

**Technical prerequisites:**
- Reliable internet connection (provider typically specifies minimum bandwidth;
  check their specs before test day)
- Webcam and microphone
- Quiet, distraction-free room
- Provider's proctoring software installed and tested (this is mandatory — run
  the system check at least 24 hours before the exam)

**Environment rules:**
- No secondary monitors or devices visible to the proctor
- No one else in the room during the exam
- No talking to others during the exam (even on mute, many proctors can see
  mouth movements)
- No external materials, notes, or open browser tabs
- Phone and smartwatch out of sight and off

**Setup before exam day:**
1. Download and install the proctoring software (Examity, Proctorio, Pearson
   OnVUE, etc. — depends on provider)
2. Run the system compatibility check (this is non-negotiable)
3. Do a full dry run with your laptop, room, webcam, and audio at least once
   — test connection, lighting, and what the proctor sees on screen
4. Bookmark the proctoring software login page for quick access on exam day
5. Restart your router and computer the morning of the exam to clear memory
   and ensure stability

### Test-center registration and rules

If you're taking the exam at a physical test center:

**Registration:**
- Confirm your test center choice when you register
- Verify it's open on your target exam date (test centers sometimes close for
  holidays)
- Note the check-in time (usually arrive 15 minutes early; some require 30 min)
- Get the address and parking information

**Test-center exam-day rules (provider-specific, but commonly enforced):**
- Bring government-issued photo ID (same name and format as registration)
- Arrive early; late arrivals may forfeit your exam fee
- No personal belongings in the testing room (lockers are provided, but verify
  at your center)
- No phones, smartwatches, or recording devices
- No food or drinks in the testing room (water is usually provided)
- You may be patted down or have your bag inspected (this is standard)
- Bathroom breaks may be monitored or timed by the proctor
- The test center has the authority to deny entry if you're not on the
  registration list or if ID doesn't match

### Rescheduling and cancellation policy

Exam providers set strict deadlines for rescheduling or canceling without
penalty. These are **provider-specific facts** you must verify in your
`exam-facts.md` or on the provider's official page:

- Typically, rescheduling requires 24–72 hours' notice before the exam
- Canceling within 24 hours usually forfeits your exam fee entirely
- Some providers offer one free reschedule; others charge a fee each time
- If you miss your exam (no-show), you forfeit your fee and must re-register
  and pay again

**What to do:** Before exam day, know your provider's exact rescheduling
deadline. If you're sick, injured, or must reschedule, contact the provider
immediately — waiting until after your scheduled time results in a no-show and
loss of fees.

## Exam-day strategy

Exam day is not the time to learn new material. It's the time to retrieve,
reason, and make decisions under time pressure. This section gives you the
tactics.

### Exam-day checklist

**The night before:**
- No heavy study; review your mistake-ledger and weak-area queue once (20–30
  min max)
- Prepare your physical setup (if online-proctored: test lighting, close other
  applications, restart your computer)
- If test-center: confirm your center's address, parking, and check-in time
- Lay out your ID and any other documents required
- Get a full night of sleep — this matters more than one extra hour of review

**The morning of the exam:**
- Eat a normal breakfast; don't try anything new
- Arrive at the test center early enough to use the bathroom and settle in
  (typically 15–30 min before start time)
- If online: log in 10 minutes early, run the system check one more time, and
  confirm your environment with the proctor

### Time-management strategy

You have a fixed exam duration (from `exam-facts.md`, Format section). The
biggest mistake candidates make is spending too much time on hard questions and
running out of time on easy points.

**Your rhythm:**

1. **First pass: questions you know** (30% of time)
   - Scan the entire exam quickly
   - Answer every question you're confident about
   - Mark your confidence level mentally (don't waste time on this — move fast)
   - This pass buys you points and psychological momentum

2. **Second pass: questions you partially understand** (40% of time)
   - Now go back to medium-difficulty or semi-familiar questions
   - Use elimination: cross out obviously wrong answers first
   - If you can narrow it to two choices, make a reasoned guess (better odds
     than random)
   - Flag these so you can revisit if time remains

3. **Third pass: genuinely unknown questions** (20% of time)
   - These are your "flag and return" items
   - Don't sink time here on the first pass — the cost is too high
   - Make an educated guess based on any pattern you see (option balance,
     common distractors from other questions), mark it, and move on

4. **Final buffer: review flagged items** (10% of time)
   - If you have time left, revisit flagged questions
   - Has context from a later question clarified an earlier one?
   - Do you have a second theory worth trying?
   - Otherwise, stick with your earlier answer (changing answers under
     pressure often makes them worse)

**Time-per-question rule of thumb:**
- If an exam has *n* questions in *m* minutes, allocate *m/n* minutes per
  question as your target
- If a question is taking more than 1.5× the target time and you're not making
  progress, flag it and move on
- Exam designers expect most questions to be answerable in the target window —
  if you're stuck, trust your gut and move on

### Handling unknown questions

You will encounter questions you don't know how to solve. That's normal. The
question is: how do you respond without panic?

**The three-move approach:**

1. **Read the question actively, not passively**
   - Underline the question being asked (some questions have long setups; the
     actual question is at the end)
   - Cross out obviously wrong answers
   - For multi-select or scenario questions, re-read to confirm you're answering
     the right thing, not just something related

2. **Use context from the exam itself**
   - Has a similar concept appeared in an earlier question, with a hint in the
     answer choices or explanation?
   - Does another question provide a clue about this one?
   - (For online exams: the questions before and after may have context clues;
     use the "review" feature if available to look back)

3. **Make an educated guess**
   - Eliminate options that are obviously wrong (too specific, contradicts
     something you know, uses unfamiliar jargon incorrectly)
   - Between remaining options: pick the one that sounds most "official,"
     complete, or specific (exam makers often use this structure; generic or
     vague answers are often distractors)
   - If still tied: pick the middle option (second or third of four) — a mild
     statistical bias in exam design, but worth a shot
   - Mark it, move on, and don't second-guess unless you have time to revisit

**What not to do:**
- Don't panic or "give up" on a question and leave it blank (a blank is always
  wrong; a guess has a chance)
- Don't spend more than 1–2 minutes on a single unknown question in the first
  pass
- Don't change your answer unless you have a concrete reason (e.g. a later
  question clarified the concept)

### Managing time and stress

**During the exam:**
- If you feel overwhelmed, pause for 10 seconds and take three slow breaths
  (this genuinely helps; proctors are used to this)
- Keep the time-management strategy in mind: first pass, second pass, third
  pass, review
- If you run out of time: flag the remaining questions and guess on them all
  (blank answers score zero; educated guesses score 25% on average in
  multiple-choice)
- Watch for pacing: if you're halfway through time with 20% of questions left,
  accelerate

**After the exam:**
- Don't immediately review your answers with study materials (this breeds
  regret for no reason)
- Write down any questions or concepts you're unsure about, not from memory
  but from what you recall being tested
- Wait 1–2 hours before reviewing; you'll have perspective

## Final 24 hours

The 24 hours before the exam are not for learning — they're for consolidation,
logistics confirmation, and mental preparation.

### What to do

**Morning of exam day minus 1 (12–24 hours before):**
- Light review of your weak-area queue or mistake-ledger (no more than 20–30
  min)
- Confirm test-center location, parking, and check-in time, or run one final
  system check for online proctoring
- Prepare: lay out ID, any required documents, and confirm your system setup
- Eat normally, hydrate, and move your body (a 20-minute walk or run helps
  clear anxiety)

**Final 2–3 hours before the exam:**
- Stop studying entirely
- If test-center: give yourself 1 hour to travel, park, find the room, and
  settle
- If online: give yourself 30 minutes to restart your computer, clear the
  room, and log in early
- Avoid new information (don't skim a study guide or watch an explanation video
  now — you'll plant doubt about things you already know)

### What not to do

- **Don't cram or review heavily** — the night before or the morning of an exam,
  only light review works. Heavy study introduces doubt, pushes out retention,
  and tires your brain
- **Don't try new study materials** — this is not the time to read a book
  chapter or watch a long explanation video you haven't seen before
- **Don't compare yourself to others** — don't call a friend and ask "did you
  study X?" This plants doubt unnecessarily
- **Don't overthink logistics** — once you've confirmed your route and setup, stop
  fiddling with it; trust your preparation
- **Don't stay up late** — exam day requires alertness, and sleep deprivation is
  a bigger performance penalty than one extra hour of review

### Mental preparation

You've done the work. This exam is a single data point on the path to mastery.
Confidence comes from practice, not from hoping you remembered everything.

- Remind yourself of one topic you studied and now understand (not one you're
  still unsure about)
- Visualize walking into the exam, reading a question, and solving it calmly
- Know that you will encounter some hard questions — that's normal and expected
- Remember: the exam is designed so most people pass it (if the pass rate is
  70%, that means the median candidate gets ~70% right, and you've prepared to
  exceed that)

The exam itself is not a judgment of you. It's a measure of readiness for a
specific role or domain. You either pass and move forward, or you don't and
iterate. Both are valuable.

## Certification-specific overrides

The logistics above are general. Your specific exam will have provider-specific
rules that override or clarify these guidelines. Before exam day, locate and
read:

1. **Your provider's official exam-day checklist** — this is gospel; it
   overrides anything here
2. **Your specific test center's rules** (if applicable) — test centers often
   have stricter rules than the provider minimum
3. **Your proctoring software's requirements** (if online) — Examity, Proctorio,
   Pearson OnVUE, and others each have different policies
4. **Any recent updates** from your provider's blog or announcement page — exam
   rules change; check within the week before your exam
